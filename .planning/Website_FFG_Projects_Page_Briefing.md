# Flying Fortress Games — Projects Page Brief

## Purpose

The Projects page is not a portfolio.

It is not a catalogue.

It is not a product page.

It is the entrance hall of Flying Fortress Games.

Visitors are not choosing software.

They are choosing a destination.

---

# Three Customers

## Georges Beems

**May 17, 1956.**

Georges Beems enters Cartier on the Place Vendôme.

He has spent months saving for a watch.

Not because he needs a watch.

Because he hopes that, one day, a young woman will look at his wrist and see the man she wishes to marry.

---

## Lord Remington

**September 5, 1938.**

Lord Remington arrives at the Rolls-Royce showroom.

He already owns fine automobiles.

Yet he spends the afternoon discussing paint, leather and walnut veneers.

The coupé must look at home beneath the roses of Remington House.

---

## Jean-Marie Le Duick

**December 13, 1948.**

Jean-Marie Le Duick walks into the Compagnie Générale Transatlantique office in Nantes.

He asks for brochures.

He is getting married in April.

After that will come work, responsibility and ordinary life.

Before all of that, there will be one voyage.

---

None of them came to buy an object.

Not the watch.

Not the automobile.

Not the ocean liner.

Each came to prepare for a new chapter of life.

The object was only the vessel.

The real destination was someone else.

Flying Fortress Games should create that same feeling.

The visitor should never feel invited to purchase a game.

The visitor should feel invited to begin a journey.

---

# Visual Philosophy

The page follows the principles established in the Flying Fortress Games Visual Brief.

Industrial optimism.

Engineering.

Craftsmanship.

Elegance.

Confidence.

Beauty emerging from proportion rather than decoration.

The atmosphere is calm.

Nothing shouts.

Nothing begs for attention.

The visitor has already decided to dream.

The page merely asks:

> Where shall we go?

# Paths

The web offers one thing that paper never could.

The carousel.

This should not be treated as a modern UI component.

It should be understood as a brochure display.

Imagine entering a CGT office in the late 1950s.

Several brochures rest upon a polished walnut counter.

Each promises a different destination.

One naturally reaches for the next.

The carousel reproduces that gesture.

Sliding left or right is equivalent to picking up another brochure.

Each slide is a cover.

Not a summary.

Not a product card.

Not a dashboard.

A cover.

The Flying Fortress Games identity remains fixed.

The brochure changes.

Every project has its own artistic identity.

Its own colours.

Its own typography.

Its own atmosphere.

The brochure hints at that world without fully revealing it.

Opening the project is equivalent to opening the brochure.

The cover should not explain.

It should create desire.

The visitor should think:

> I want to know what lies beyond this cover.

---

# Structure

The page should contain very little information.

Each project should communicate through:

- a large cover image or illustration
- a project title
- a short line establishing atmosphere
- an invitation to enter

Nothing else should compete for attention.

The interaction should feel deliberate.

Browsing.

Not scrolling.

Discovering.

Not consuming.

---

# Things To Avoid

- Product cards
- Feature lists
- Steam page aesthetics
- Startup landing pages
- App-store design
- Excessive calls to action
- Marketing language
- Loud visual effects

---

# Final Test

If a visitor leaves the page thinking:

> "Those look like three interesting games."

The design has failed.

If the visitor leaves thinking:

> "I wonder what life awaits me there."

The page has succeeded.

## Entering a Project

The transition from the Projects page to a project should feel like crossing a threshold.

Paper could only suggest the journey.

The web can begin it.

The interaction is therefore part of the narrative.

Examples include:

- A camera slowly approaching a moored ocean liner before boarding.
- Entering a station concourse through its main doors.
- Walking beneath an aircraft wing towards the cabin stairs.
- Approaching a château before entering the courtyard.

The visitor should not feel like they clicked a hyperlink.

They should feel like they crossed into another world.

The exact interaction remains to be designed and may differ between desktop and mobile.
